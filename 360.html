What Is 360° Photography?

Photography has many different forms, some people use film, some people use digital and some people even use a demon in a box that draws the pictures for you... however, this isn't Discworld and the imaging techniques used aren't going to be that simple...or magical. 360° photography is an imaging technique that has been applied in many different medias and situations as its uses can present a large amount of detail in a single area. Its ability to put a viewer in the centre of a scene means one can pan up down, left, and right, Giving the viewers the experience of being within the image looking around. These 360° images are a type of panorama (images stitched together in a line) which have had many ways of being captured and presented since its first use. Varying successes and weaknesses have been noted as technology in this area of photography has evolved.

A Brief History Of 360° Photography?

There is not much information about the introduction of 360° imaging as its use has only started to take off over the last two decades. There have been quite a few ways of producing spherical panoramas starting from the 1990's release of the Noblex Panoramic cameras, which had a lens that panned 136° to capture a long panorama on one piece of film.

Another 360° imaging technique called the Kaidan 360 VR One, a lens developed between companies Kaidan and Eyesee360 and offers a 'complete 360° image in a single snapshot', however this single shot is presented in the form of a 'doughnut' due to the lens optics but with computer effects can be made to look like a spherical image.

Due to the complexities of these panoramic methods the Noblex and Kaidan are not cheap, costing between £750 and £1000 brand new however both the Noblex and Kaidan are no longer manufactured.

During the 2010's other companies such as LG, Samsung, Insta360 and Ricoh have all released their submission for portable consumer all in one 360 cameras where all of the image capture and post process in done in camera, costing from £150 for the LG360 Cam up to £300 for the Ricoh Theta and even £400 for the Insta360 One X. Like most technology 360° imaging cameras rise up in quality as you pay more money, with the Insta360 One X offering up a 18 megapixel image with a resolution of 6080 x 3040 whereas the cheaper LG 360 Cam only offering up 13 megapixel images with image resolutions of 5,660 x 2,830, with technology so rapidly evolving, it is safe to say that today's new and latest ideas, will be tomorrows history.
